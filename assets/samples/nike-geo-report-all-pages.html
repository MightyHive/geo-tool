Executive summary

Our generative engine optimization audit of Nike evaluated a representative sample of 40 pages, resulting in a weak overall score of 55.0 out of 100. The most critical finding is that Nike's AI visibility is severely restricted because several key pages are marked with noindex tags, which completely blocks AI crawlers from indexing and citing the brand's content. This technical barrier directly impacts Nike's bottom line by ceding valuable real estate in AI-driven search results to competitors, ultimately reducing organic traffic and brand authority. To reclaim this search share, Nike must immediately update crawler access rules in robots.txt to permit AI search engines to read public pages. Additionally, the brand should expand Organization structured data with verified sameAs profile links to solidify brand identity, and optimize content with passage-level answerability patterns like direct definitions and FAQs that AI engines can easily extract and cite.

Technical setup81.5Good(30%)

robots.txt is a simple instruction file at the root of your site that tells crawlers (including many AI crawlers) which URLs they may fetch. We could retrieve yours—still audit it whenever you launch new sections, and avoid overly broad “block everything” rules that can hide you from AI search results.

The technical checks in this report look at whether robots.txt was available, whether sampled pages returned a normal successful response (we report this as an HTTP status such as 200—meaning the page answered OK), and whether our crawl used a trusted HTTPS connection. Core Web Vitals (real-user loading and layout stability) and time to first byte (how quickly the server starts responding) matter for visitors and search quality, but they are not measured in this automated run—use your analytics toolkit or a dedicated speed test for those.

E-E-A-T stands for Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, and Trustworthiness—how well your content demonstrates real-world experience, subject-matter depth, credible sourcing, and reliability. Search and AI systems use these signals to decide what to surface and cite. The scores below are approximate indicators from this crawl; they complement—not replace—a full editorial and fact-check review.

53/100

Experience is about whether pages read like they were written by people who have actually done the job, used the product, or lived the situation—not just generic filler. Search and AI systems reward depth, specificity, and helpful detail that sounds first-hand.

42/100

Expertise is how convincingly you demonstrate knowledge: clear explanations, useful structure, and cues that a knowledgeable author or organization stands behind the content. AI cites sources that look like they know the topic end-to-end.

How we calculate this number: We take 45% of your content quality & structure score, then add 15 points when structured data was detected (it helps machines understand entities and facts) and 5 points when a live llms.txt file exists (a small discoverability signal for AI tools). Authoritativeness Can others (and machines) verify who you are?
31/100

Authoritativeness is recognition and corroboration: consistent branding, visible footprint, and links that tie your site to trusted profiles elsewhere. When those line up, readers and models treat you as a real entity worth citing.

How we calculate this number: We take 60% of your brand / entity visibility score from this audit, then add 15 points when structured data includes sameAs links to official profiles (for example social or knowledge-base URLs you control). Trust Do the basics feel safe, maintained, and accessible?
78/100

Trust covers the hygiene people notice before they read a word: secure browsing, pages that load without errors, and sensible rules for bots. It is not a legal or financial audit—just whether automated checks saw a well-maintained, reachable site.

How we calculate this number: We blend 55% of your technical setup score with 25% of your AI crawler access score, then add 10 points when HTTPS/TLS looked healthy in this crawl and 5 points when llms.txt was present. This crawl also applied: HTTPS/TLS looked healthy (+10).
